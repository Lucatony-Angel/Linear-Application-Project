Methods — Linear Algebra Tools

1Variable Definitions

For each team $i$ we define the primary predictor as the fraction of squad players who played in one of the five major European leagues:

$$p_i = \frac{\text{num\_in\_top5}_i}{\text{squad\_size}_i} \in [0,1]$$

The response variable is a numeric success score based on stage reached:

  • 1 = Group Stage   2 = Round of 16   3 = Quarterfinal
  • 4 = Semifinal   5 = Finalist   6 = Champion

A second predictor is the FIFA ranking $r_i$ (October 2022). Note that a lower ranking number indicates a stronger team, so we expect a negative correlation with stage reached.

2Correlation Matrix

We stack four variables into an $n \times 4$ data matrix $M$ (pct_in_top5, fifa_ranking, stage_reached, goal_diff), then mean-center each column:

$$\tilde{M} = M - \mathbf{1}\bar{\mathbf{x}}^\top \qquad (\bar{\mathbf{x}} = \text{column-mean vector})$$

The sample covariance matrix is a single matrix product — this is the key linear algebra step:

$$C = \frac{1}{n-1}\,\tilde{M}^\top \tilde{M} \in \mathbb{R}^{4\times 4}$$

Normalising by the outer product of standard deviations gives the Pearson correlation matrix $R$, with all entries in $[-1,1]$:

$$R_{ij} = \frac{C_{ij}}{\sigma_i\,\sigma_j}, \qquad \sigma_i = \sqrt{C_{ii}}$$

With $n=32$ teams, $C$ is $4\times 4$ and symmetric. Its diagonal is all ones by construction. See the Results page for the computed matrix.

3Least-Squares via Normal Equations

We seek $\boldsymbol{\beta} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ that minimises the squared residual between the linear model and the observed stages $y$:

$$\min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\;\|y - X\boldsymbol{\beta}\|^2, \qquad X = \begin{bmatrix}1 & p_1 & r_1 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ 1 & p_{n} & r_{n}\end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{32\times 3}$$

Setting $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\|y-X\boldsymbol{\beta}\|^2 = 0$ yields the normal equations:

$$X^\top X\,\boldsymbol{\beta} = X^\top y \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \boldsymbol{\beta} = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y$$

Here $X^\top X$ is a $3\times 3$ symmetric positive-definite matrix, so the solution is unique and computed exactly via numpy.linalg.solve — no iteration required.

4Goodness of Fit (R²)

We measure how much variance in stage reached the model explains using:

$$R^2 = 1 - \frac{\|y - \hat{y}\|^2}{\|y - \bar{y}\mathbf{1}\|^2} = 1 - \frac{SS_{\text{res}}}{SS_{\text{tot}}}$$

$R^2=1$ means a perfect fit; $R^2=0$ means the model does no better than simply predicting the mean for every team.
