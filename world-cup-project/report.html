Methods — Linear Algebra Tools

1Variable Definitions

For each team we define the predictor as the fraction of squad players who played in one of the five major European leagues:

$$\text{pct in top5}_i = \frac{\text{num in top5}_i}{\text{squad size}_i}$$

The response variable is a numeric success score based on stage reached:

  • 1 = Group Stage    2 = Round of 16    3 = Quarterfinal
  • 4 = Semifinal    5 = Finalist    6 = Champion

2Correlation Matrix

We stack four variables into an $n \times 4$ data matrix $M$, then mean-center each column to get $\tilde M$:

$$\tilde{M} = M - \mathbf{1}\bar{\mathbf{x}}^\top \qquad (\bar{\mathbf{x}} \text{ is the column-mean vector})$$

The sample covariance matrix is then computed via a single matrix product:

$$C = \frac{1}{n-1}\,\tilde{M}^\top \tilde{M}$$

Dividing by the outer product of standard deviations gives the correlation matrix, whose entries lie in $[-1, 1]$:

$$r_{ij} = \frac{C_{ij}}{\sigma_i\,\sigma_j} \qquad \sigma_i = \sqrt{C_{ii}}$$

With $n = 32$ teams and four variables, $C$ is a $4\times 4$ symmetric matrix. The diagonal entries are all $1$ by construction.

3Least-Squares via Normal Equations

We seek the coefficient vector $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ that minimises the sum of squared residuals between the model $\hat{y} = X\boldsymbol{\beta}$ and the observed stages $y$:

$$\min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\;\|\,y - X\boldsymbol{\beta}\|^2 \qquad X = \begin{bmatrix}1 & p_1 & r_1 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ 1 & p_n & r_n\end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{32\times 3}$$

Setting the gradient to zero yields the normal equations, which we solve exactly:

$$X^\top X\,\boldsymbol{\beta} = X^\top y \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \boldsymbol{\beta} = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y$$

Here $p_i$ = pct in top‑5 and $r_i$ = FIFA ranking for team $i$. Because $X^\top X$ is a $3\times 3$ positive-definite matrix, the solution is unique.

Conclusion & Limitations

What the model found

There is a moderate positive correlation between a team's top‑5 league share and its World Cup stage (r = 0.489). FIFA ranking is a slightly stronger individual predictor (r = -0.501, negative because a lower ranking number means a stronger team). Together the two predictors explain 28.9% of the variance in stage reached (R² = 0.289).

The regression coefficient on pct_in_top5 is 1.118, meaning a team with 100% of players in top‑5 leagues is predicted to reach roughly 1.12 stages further than a team with 0%—holding FIFA ranking constant. Each one‑place drop in FIFA ranking reduces the predicted stage by 0.025.

Limitations

  • Small sample: Only 32 data points makes estimates noisy. A few upset results (e.g., Saudi Arabia beating Argentina in group stage) can move the regression line noticeably.
  • Coarse response variable: Stage reached is ordinal, not continuous—jumping from Round‑of‑16 to Quarterfinal is not the same magnitude as Champion to Finalist, yet the model treats them equally.
  • Collinearity: pct_in_top5 and FIFA ranking are themselves correlated (r = -0.694), so their individual coefficients are harder to interpret cleanly.
  • Omitted variables: Injuries, draw luck, home‑continent advantage (Morocco, Qatar), and tactical coaching all affect outcomes but are not captured.
  • Top‑5 definition: Playing in a top‑5 league does not guarantee playing time. A bench player at PSG contributes less than a starter at a mid‑table club.

Takeaway

The linear algebra tools—correlation matrices and least‑squares via normal equations—give a clean quantitative picture: top‑5 league experience is meaningfully associated with World Cup success, but it explains only about a third of the variation. Tournament football remains genuinely unpredictable, and that is precisely what makes it worth watching.
